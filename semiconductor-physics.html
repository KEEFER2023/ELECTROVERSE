43. What is a Semiconductor?

A semiconductor is a material whose electrical conductivity lies between that of a conductor and an insulator. Examples include silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge).

Conductivity: œÉ (metal) >> œÉ (semiconductor) >> œÉ (insulator)
Semiconductors become conductive when thermal energy excites electrons across a band gap.

44. Energy Band Theory

  • Valence Band (VB): Filled with electrons
  • Conduction Band (CB): Where free electrons can move
  • Band Gap (Eg): Energy needed to jump from VB to CB

45. Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Semiconductors

  • Intrinsic: Pure Si or Ge
  • Extrinsic: Doped to increase carriers

Doping adds donor (n-type) or acceptor (p-type) atoms.

Example: Adding phosphorus (5 valence e‚Åª) to Si gives n-type; adding boron (3 valence e‚Åª) gives p-type.

46. Charge Carriers

Two types of mobile charge carriers:

  • Electrons: Negative, from donor atoms or CB
  • Holes: Positive, absence of e‚Åª in VB
Current = electron flow + hole flow

47. The pn Junction

Formed by joining p-type and n-type semiconductors:

  • Electrons from n side diffuse to p side
  • Holes from p side diffuse to n side
  • This forms a depletion region with built-in potential
Forward bias reduces barrier ‚Üí current flows
Reverse bias widens barrier ‚Üí current blocked

49. Transistors (BJT & FET)

  • BJT: Uses base-emitter junction to control collector current
  • FET: Controls channel conductivity via gate voltage
BJTs amplify current; FETs act as voltage-controlled switches.

50. Semiconductor Applications

  • Transistors: Amplifiers, switches
  • Solar cells: Light-to-electricity
