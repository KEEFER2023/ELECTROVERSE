1. Introduction to Electric Fields

An electric field is the region in space around a charged object where other charged objects experience a force. It's a fundamental concept in both physics and electrical engineering, governing how charges interact, how capacitors store energy, and how electromagnetic waves begin.

Definition: The electric field E at a point in space is defined as the force F experienced by a test charge q placed at that point: E = F / q

The SI unit of the electric field is Newtons per Coulomb (N/C) or equivalently Volts per meter (V/m).

Always think of electric fields as vectors. Their direction matters — it’s the direction a positive test charge would be pushed.

2. Electric Field Due to a Point Charge

From Coulomb’s Law, the force between two point charges is:

F = (1 / 4πε₀) × (|qQ| / r²)

This gives the electric field due to a point charge Q at distance r:

E = (1 / 4πε₀) × (|Q| / r²)
  • ε₀ = 8.854 × 10-12 C2/N·m2 (vacuum permittivity)
  • The field points away from positive charges and toward negative charges
Example: What is the electric field 0.2 m away from a +3 μC charge? E = (9 × 10⁹)(3 × 10⁻⁶) / (0.2)^2 = 6.75 × 10⁵ N/C

3. Superposition Principle

Electric fields obey the principle of superposition. If multiple charges affect a point, the total electric field is the vector sum of the fields from each charge.

Example: Two charges +2 μC at x=0 and -2 μC at x=4 m. What is the net field at x=2 m?

Use individual field magnitudes and directions, then apply vector addition (in this case, they add).

4. Electric Dipoles

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges +q and -q separated by a distance 2a. The dipole moment is:

p = q × 2a

The electric field at a distant point on the axial line of the dipole is:

E = (1 / 4πε₀) × (2p / r3)

At a point on the perpendicular bisector (equatorial line):

E = (1 / 4πε₀) × (p / r3)
Dipole fields drop off faster with distance than point charges (proportional to 1/r3).
Dipoles are used in antenna design, molecular physics, and field sensors.

6. Continuous Charge Distributions

When dealing with objects that have charge spread over a length, surface, or volume, the electric field is calculated using integration. Depending on the type of distribution, we use different forms:

  • Linear: λ = charge per unit length → dE = (1 / 4πε₀) × (λ dx / r²)
  • Surface: σ = charge per unit area → dE = (1 / 4πε₀) × (σ dA / r²)
  • Volume: ρ = charge per unit volume → dE = (1 / 4πε₀) × (ρ dV / r²)
Example: Find the electric field at a point on the axis of a uniformly charged rod.

Set up a coordinate system and integrate over the length of the rod using symmetry and the linear charge density λ.

Look for symmetry to simplify direction and integration limits.

7. Gauss’s Law

Gauss's Law is a powerful tool to compute electric fields when symmetry is present. It states:

∮ E · dA = Q_enc / ε₀

This means the total electric flux through a closed surface equals the net enclosed charge divided by ε₀.

Best used with spherical, cylindrical, or planar symmetry.
Example (Spherical): Find the electric field outside a uniformly charged sphere. E = (1 / 4πε₀)(Q / r²), r > R

Same form as a point charge.

Example (Cylindrical): Infinite line of charge λ. E = λ / (2πε₀r)

Apply cylindrical Gaussian surface.

8. Conductors in Electrostatics

  • Electric field inside a conductor is always zero
  • Excess charge resides on the surface
  • Field just outside is perpendicular to the surface: E = σ / ε₀
  • Conductors can shield internal regions from external electric fields (Faraday cage)
Used in designing enclosures for sensitive electronics (e.g., in high-voltage labs).

9. Real-World Engineering Applications

  • High-voltage insulation: Design based on breakdown limits of air and dielectrics.
  • Capacitor design: Field uniformity and energy storage based on E = V/d.
  • Electrostatic Precipitators: Use field to remove particles from gases.
  • Semiconductor Junctions: Built-in electric fields control carrier movement.
  • EM Wave Generation: Starts from oscillating electric fields (Maxwell’s equations).

10. Practice Questions

Q1: What is the field 1 m from a +5 μC charge?

Answer: E = (9×10⁹)(5×10⁻⁶)/1² = 4.5×10⁴ N/C

Q2: Use Gauss's Law for a spherical charge Q. What’s E outside?

Answer: E = (1 / 4πε₀)(Q / r²)

Q3: Why is E=0 inside a conductor?

Answer: Charges rearrange to cancel internal field (electrostatic equilibrium).
