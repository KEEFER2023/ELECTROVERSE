⚡ Energy and Power in Electromagnetic Systems

This section explains how energy is stored, transferred, and converted in electromagnetic systems — covering core equations and engineering use cases.

2.6. Electric Power Basics

P = V × I — The instantaneous power delivered in a circuit. In AC systems, consider root mean square (RMS) values:

P_avg = V_rms × I_rms × cos(ϕ)
ϕ is the phase angle between voltage and current. cos(ϕ) is called the power factor.

2.7. Energy Stored in Magnetic Fields

Inductors store energy in their magnetic fields:

E = (1/2) L I²

Where L is inductance in henries, I current in amperes.

2.8. Energy Density in Fields

  • Electric field energy density: u_E = (1/2) ε E²
  • Magnetic field energy density: u_B = (1/2μ) B²
Energy is spread throughout the space around fields — not just at the source!

2.9. The Poynting Vector

\vec{S} = \vec{E} \times \vec{H} — The power flow per unit area in electromagnetic fields.

Direction of S gives energy propagation direction; magnitude gives intensity (W/m²).

3.0. Conservation of Energy in Electromagnetic Fields

Poynting’s theorem (differential form):

∂u/∂t + ∇·S = -J · E

This equates the change in field energy + outgoing flux to work done on charges.

3.1. AC Power: Real, Reactive, Apparent

  • Real Power (P): Actually consumed power → P = V_rms I_rms cos(ϕ)
  • Reactive Power (Q): Stored and returned → Q = V_rms I_rms sin(ϕ)
  • Apparent Power (S): Total supplied → S = V_rms × I_rms
S² = P² + Q²

3.2. Power in Inductive and Capacitive Loads

  • Pure inductor: Voltage leads current by 90°, Q positive
  • Pure capacitor: Current leads voltage by 90°, Q negative
  • Resistive load: No phase difference, Q = 0

3.3. Practice Problems

Q1: A 0.5 H inductor with 2 A current. Stored energy?

Answer: E = 0.5 × 0.5 × 2² = 1 J

Q2: V_rms = 230 V, I_rms = 5 A, ϕ = 30°

Real power: P = 230×5×cos(30°) ≈ 996 W

Q3: Capacitor in AC circuit draws 2 A at 100 V

Reactive power: Q = 100×2 = 200 VAR
