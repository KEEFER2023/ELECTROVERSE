🔄 Thevenin & Norton Theorems

Thevenin’s and Norton’s theorems simplify complex linear circuits to basic equivalent models, making analysis far easier — especially when focusing on a particular component or load.

1. 📘 Thevenin’s Theorem

Any linear two-terminal circuit can be reduced to a single voltage source Vth in series with a resistance Rth.

🔧 Steps to Apply Thevenin’s Theorem:

  1. Remove the load resistor.
  2. Find Vth: the open-circuit voltage across terminals.
  3. Find Rth: deactivate sources (V→0, I→open) and calculate equivalent resistance.
  4. Reconnect the load to this simplified circuit.
Example: Find Thevenin equivalent across a 10Ω load.
Given: 12V source in series with 4Ω, then parallel with 6Ω. Load is across the 6Ω.
Vth = 12 × (6 / (4+6)) = 7.2V
Rth = (4‖6) = (4×6)/(4+6) = 2.4Ω
Animated Concept: Replace a complex network with Vth and Rth th

2. 🔁 Norton’s Theorem

Norton’s Theorem is the dual of Thevenin’s. Any linear two-terminal network can be simplified to a current source IN in parallel with resistance RN.

🔧 Steps to Apply Norton’s Theorem:

  1. Remove the load.
  2. Find IN: short the output and calculate short-circuit current.
  3. Find RN: same method as Rth.
  4. Reconnect the load across the simplified Norton equivalent.
Example: Use Norton to simplify the same 12V–4Ω–6Ω setup.
IN = V / Req = 12 / 10 = 1.2 A
RN = Rth = 2.4 Ω
Animated Concept: Network becomes IN in parallel with RN N

3. 🔄 Thevenin ↔ Norton Equivalence

You can convert between Thevenin and Norton forms:

  • Vth = IN × RN
  • IN = Vth / Rth
Thevenin and Norton equivalents are interchangeable. Use the one that simplifies your calculations best.

4. 📝 Practice Quiz

Q: What's the Thevenin equivalent of a 10V source in series with 5Ω?

Ans: Vth = 10V, Rth = 5Ω

Q: Convert Thevenin (10V, 5Ω) to Norton.

Ans: IN = 10 / 5 = 2 A, RN = 5Ω

Q: Why deactivate sources to find Rth?

Ans: It isolates resistance effects of the network.
