1. ⚡ Ohm's Law – Foundation of Circuit Behavior

Ohm’s Law defines the linear relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in a conductor. It is a fundamental principle used throughout all of circuit analysis.

V = I × R
  • V (Voltage): Electric potential difference in volts (V)
  • I (Current): Flow of charge in amperes (A)
  • R (Resistance): Opposition to current in ohms (Ω)

🧠 Deep Insight

From a microscopic perspective, Ohm’s Law arises due to electron collisions in the lattice of the conductor. The resistance depends on material properties, cross-section, and temperature.

Key Material Insight: Resistivity (ρ) is intrinsic to the material:

R = ρ × (L / A)
  • ρ = resistivity (Ω·m)
  • L = length of the conductor (m)
  • A = cross-sectional area (m²)
Example: A copper wire of 1.5 m length and 1 mm² cross-section. ρ = 1.68×10⁻⁸ Ω·m.
R = ρ × (L/A) = 1.68×10⁻⁸ × (1.5 / 1×10⁻⁶) = 0.0252 Ω

📐 Graphical Interpretation

Ohmic devices produce a linear V-I plot. The slope equals resistance (R). Non-ohmic components like diodes show nonlinear curves.

⚙️ Real-Life Engineering Application

In circuit design, engineers use Ohm’s Law to:

  • Size resistors for LEDs
  • Limit current to microcontrollers
  • Balance current paths in power circuits
Example: An LED needs 20 mA and has a forward drop of 2V. The power supply is 9V. What resistor to use?
V_R = 9V - 2V = 7V → R = V/I = 7V / 0.02A = 350 Ω

🧮 Power Considerations

P = V × I = I² × R = V² / R
Example: A 2 Ω resistor carries 1.5 A.
P = I² × R = (1.5)² × 2 = 2.25 × 2 = 4.5 W → Use a 5W resistor.

📝 Practice Quiz

Q: A device draws 0.25 A from a 9 V source. What’s its resistance?

Ans: R = V / I = 9 / 0.25 = 36 Ω

Q: A 10 Ω resistor has 2 A flowing through it. Find the voltage.

Ans: V = I × R = 2 × 10 = 20 V

Q: A carbon resistor labeled 470Ω is used with a 12V supply. What current flows?

Ans: I = V / R = 12 / 470 ≈ 25.5 mA
